STEADYWRK Trainer Run Sheet

Private screen. Share only the animated deck. Keep pace calm and practical.

Goal: alert -> tech -> update -> proof -> SCOA

0. Before You Start

"Today is practical. We are going to walk through how a dispatcher handles a real work order: read it, find a technician, make the call, update the team, use AI carefully, and close with proof. This also shows what our IGNITE FOUNDATION program teaches and what the SCOA certificate proves."
Set ToneClear, calm, no hype. They should feel: "I can learn this."
Define SCOASTEADYWRK Certified Operations Associate.
Repeat Often"This is a job skill, not just a slide."

1. STEADYWRK Dispatch + SCOA

"The point of this training is simple: become useful in an operations room. Useful means you can read the job, speak clearly, find help, update people, and leave proof. SCOA is the certificate that says you practiced those skills to a standard."
Ask: "What do you think a dispatcher actually controls?" Let them answer. Then say: "Information, timing, technician choice, updates, and proof."

2. The Model

"A work order comes in with a budget. We call that the NTE, meaning 'not to exceed.' If the NTE is 450 dollars, the job should not go over 450 unless approval is given. We find a local technician who can do the work inside that budget. If we choose badly, we lose time and money."
Do: Give the example: "NTE is $450. Tech asks for $300. What is left?" Keep it simple. They need the logic, not accounting.

3. Your Loop

"This is the loop: alert, read, source, call, secure, update, proof, close. Say it with me once: alert, read, source, call, secure, update, proof, close."
Ask: "Which step happens before calling?" Correct answer: read the work order. "Which step protects payment?" Correct answer: proof.
Cert tie: This loop is what IGNITE FOUNDATION trains again and again until it becomes natural.

4. The Work Order

"Before any call, we read six things: location, trade, scope, urgency, NTE, and proof required. Scope means what the job is asking for. SLA means the time expectation. Proof means what we need to show the work happened."
Do on screen: Point to the sample ticket. Make them answer: city, trade, NTE, problem, proof required.
Say: "If you cannot explain the job in one sentence, you are not ready to call."

5. Opening Call

"The opening should be short and normal. You are not selling a dream. You are checking if a local technician can help today."

Read Together

"Hey, good morning, I'm [Name]. Looking for a plumber in Utica for a small commercial job today."
Do: You read it once. They repeat it. Correct only three things: city, trade, and today.

6. Qualification

"Now we check fit. Can they do the trade? Can they go today? What time? Can they send before and after photos? Do they understand this is commercial? This should take less than one minute."
"Quick one: commercial restroom sink clog. Can you take a look today?"
"What time can you arrive? And you can send before and after photos, correct?"
Ask: "What are we checking here?" Answer: skill, availability, proof, and basic professionalism.

7. Locking The Tech

"Once they are interested, then we give the basic rules. Photos are required. Updates are required. If the scope changes, they stop and tell us first. We do not let them create surprise charges."
"Perfect. I'll text the address and job notes now. Reply with ETA when you are on the way."
Simple rule: First get interest. Then explain rules. If you start with rules, the call feels heavy.

8. Updates

"A good update tells the team what happened and what happens next. Bad update: 'called tech.' Good update: 'JOB-7018: called 4 plumbers, one available at 2:30, waiting for photo confirmation, next call in 10 minutes.'"
Do: Make them write one update out loud using this format: JOB - action - status - next step.
SCOA tie: Clear updates are part of war-room discipline.

9. Scheduling

"Backup is good. Confusion is bad. If you send two technicians at the same time, you create a new problem. Keep spacing. Know who is primary and who is backup."
Ask: "Why can two techs at the same time be bad?" Answer: double charge, site confusion, angry techs, bad client experience.

11. AI Copilot

"AI can help, but it cannot be trusted blindly. Use it to summarize, translate, make a checklist, or draft an update. But always check the real source: address, scope, NTE, SLA, client rules, tech status, and proof needed."
Say this clearly: "Prompt. Verify. Decide. Never prompt, copy, send."
Example: "AI says this is low priority. But the ticket says water is backing up in a store restroom. We check the ticket, not the AI confidence."

12. What They Get

"What you leave with is practical: better operations English, better call control, better ticket reading, better local sourcing, safer AI use, cleaner updates, and proof discipline. These are skills hotels, facility teams, contractors, and operations desks actually need."
Ask: "Which skill sounds most useful to you: English, AI, sourcing, updates, or proof?" Use their answer to make it personal.

13. Script Walkthrough

"Now we practice the technician call. I will read one line. You repeat it. We are not chasing a perfect accent. We are chasing clear meaning."
1. "Hey, good morning, I'm [Name]. Looking for a plumber in Utica for a small commercial job today."
2. "Quick one: commercial restroom sink clog. Can you take a look today?"
3. "What time can you arrive? And you can send before and after photos, correct?"
4. "Perfect. I'll text the address and job notes now. Reply with ETA when you are on the way."
Do: After they repeat, ask: "What did we learn from the tech?" They should say: availability, ETA, photo agreement, phone number.

Close

"That is the work: read clearly, call clearly, update clearly, use AI carefully, and close with proof. If you can do that under pressure, you are not just watching operations. You are useful inside operations. That is what SCOA is meant to prove."
