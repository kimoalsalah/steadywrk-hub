STEADYWRK Presenter Script

Private screen. Operational tone. Clear, serious, experienced.

Control the sequence. Protect the record.

Trainer Positioning

"I am going to keep this practical. Dispatch is not just answering phones. It is controlling the sequence between the client problem, the technician, the site, and the record. If that sequence is clean, the job moves. If the sequence is messy, the job slows down, costs more, or becomes hard to prove."
Your toneCalm, direct, competent. Do not over-explain.
Training promiseThey will understand the dispatch sequence, the call flow, updates, proof, and the certificate path.
Certificate angleSCOA is proof of operating habits, not a decoration.
Critical ruleIf information is missing, verify it. Do not guess.

Opening

"Welcome. Today we are walking through the dispatcher workflow the way it actually happens: a work order comes in, we read it, we source the right technician, we make the call, we confirm timing, we update the team, and we close with proof. I will also show where this connects to IGNITE FOUNDATION and the SCOA certificate path."
"The goal is not to memorize slides. The goal is to understand the order of operations and the judgment behind it."

Slide 1 - What The Training Covers

"This training sits at the center of field-service operations. You need enough English to handle job language, enough dispatch logic to read the ticket, enough call control to secure a technician, enough discipline to update the record, and enough judgment to use tools like AI without letting them make decisions for you."
Ask: "When a job goes badly, where does it usually break: bad information, wrong technician, bad timing, or missing proof?" Let them answer, then connect it back to the slides.

Slide 2 - The Model

"The commercial logic is simple. A job comes in with a budget. That budget is usually controlled by an NTE, which means not-to-exceed. If the NTE is 450, we do not treat 450 as a suggestion. We either stay inside it or get approval before going beyond it."
"The dispatcher affects margin by choosing the right technician, confirming the price or visit terms clearly, and avoiding preventable confusion."
Use example: "NTE 450. Technician expects 300. That can work. Technician expects 600. We need approval or another option."

Slide 3 - The Dispatch Loop

"This is the desk rhythm: alert, read, source, call, confirm, update, proof, close. Every part matters. If you skip reading, the call is weak. If you skip updates, the team loses visibility. If you skip proof, closeout becomes difficult."
Main point: "A dispatcher does not just move fast. A dispatcher moves in the right order."

Slide 4 - Reading The Work Order

"Before calling, pull out the operating facts: location, trade, scope, urgency, NTE, site instructions, and proof required. Scope means what the job is asking for. ETA means estimated time of arrival. Proof means the photos, notes, invoice, or confirmation needed to close the job."
On screen: Point to the sample ticket and make them identify location, trade, NTE, problem, and proof requirement.
Say: "If you cannot explain the job in one clean sentence, you are not ready to make the call."

Slide 5 - Opening The Call

"The opening should sound normal and specific. You are not reading a paragraph. You are checking whether a local technician can handle a real job today."
"Hey, good morning, I'm [Name]. Looking for a plumber in Utica for a small commercial job today."
Practice: Read it once. Have them repeat it. Correct only clarity: city, trade, today, commercial job.

Slide 6 - Qualifying The Technician

"Now qualify the fit. Do they do this trade? Can they go today? What time? Can they handle a commercial site? Can they send before and after photos? Are they independent or owner-operated? The point is to avoid sending someone who sounds interested but cannot actually complete the job."
"Quick one: commercial restroom sink clog. Can you take a look today?"
"What time can you arrive? And you can send before and after photos, correct?"

Slide 7 - Confirming And Controlling Scope

"Once the technician is interested, confirm the next action. Send the address and notes. Get the ETA. Make sure they understand that if the scope changes, they need to stop and tell us before doing extra work. That prevents surprise charges and messy closeouts."
"Perfect. I'll text the address and job notes now. Reply with ETA when you are on the way."
Important: Do not bury the tech in rules before they are interested. First establish the job. Then confirm expectations.

Slide 8 - Updates

"An update should let another person continue the job without calling you for context. 'Called tech' is not an update. A useful update includes the job ID, action taken, current status, blocker if any, and next step."
Good update: "JOB-7018: called 4 plumbers. One available at 2:30. Waiting for photo confirmation. Next call in 10 minutes."
Practice: Have them build one update using: JOB - action - status - next step.

Slide 9 - Scheduling And Backup

"A backup technician is useful only if the schedule is controlled. If two technicians arrive together, you create confusion, possible double cost, and a bad site experience. Know who is primary, who is backup, and what time each person is expected."
Ask: "What information should be in the record when a backup is set?" Answer: name, phone, ETA, role, and next confirmation time.

Slide 10 - IGNITE FOUNDATION And SCOA

"IGNITE FOUNDATION is the broader training path behind this. It is six weeks, two sessions per week, in Aqaba and Amman, with twenty seats per cohort. It covers operations English, dispatch fundamentals, technician calls, sourcing, digital tools, AI tools, professional conduct, and a capstone simulation."
"Graduates receive SCOA, the STEADYWRK Certified Operations Associate certificate. The point of the certificate is to show that the person has practiced real operating behaviors: reading tickets, communicating clearly, updating the record, using tools carefully, and closing with proof."
Arabic if useful: "أساسيات الحقبة الذكية تدريب عملي لتعلّم تشغيل غرفة العمليات واستخدام الأدوات الحديثة بطريقة صحيحة."

Slide 11 - AI Tools

"AI tools are useful, but they are not a source of truth. Use them for summaries, translation help, checklists, or draft updates. Then verify against the actual work order. If the ticket says water is backing up, but an AI summary makes it sound low priority, trust the ticket."
Plain rule: "Use AI for help. Check the real ticket before using the answer."

Slide 12 - What They Leave With

"The practical value is this: stronger operations English, cleaner call control, better ticket reading, local sourcing habits, safer use of AI tools, better updates, and proof discipline. These are useful in dispatch, hotel operations, facility management, construction support, and field-service coordination."
Ask: "Which part do you think is hardest in real life: finding the tech, controlling the call, updating, or getting proof?" Use their answer to start discussion.

Slide 13 - Call Script Walkthrough

"Now we will practice the technician call. I will read each line once. Then you repeat it. I am not listening for a perfect accent. I am listening for clear meaning, correct job details, and a calm tone."
1. "Hey, good morning, I'm [Name]. Looking for a plumber in Utica for a small commercial job today."
2. "Quick one: commercial restroom sink clog. Can you take a look today?"
3. "What time can you arrive? And you can send before and after photos, correct?"
4. "Perfect. I'll text the address and job notes now. Reply with ETA when you are on the way."
After practice: Ask what was confirmed: trade, city, availability, ETA, photo requirement, and next step.

Close

"That is the operating standard: read the job correctly, call clearly, confirm details, update the record, use tools carefully, and close with proof. When those habits are consistent, the person is useful on a real operations desk. That is what the SCOA path is meant to represent."
If you get a question you cannot answer: "Good question. I do not want to guess. I will confirm and come back to you."
